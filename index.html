01

Frontier AI needs a connection. A large, funded market can't have one.

The best models live behind an API. If your data can't go to that API — attorney–client privilege, PHI, MNPI, classified, proprietary IP — or you're simply disconnected (a SCIF, an isolated OT network, a ship, a field site), you're locked out. And "secured cloud" doesn't save you: in-VPC, zero-retention, and confidential-computing (TEE) all still need a connection — they fail the one test that matters, does it work with the cable unplugged?

Who's locked out

Anyone who needs frontier quality but must run offline / air-gapped — by law, contract, classification, sovereignty, a disconnected environment, or a no-vendor-dependency mandate. Legal, quant/finance, government & defense, IP-sensitive R&D, healthcare, and every field / edge / OT site the cloud can't reach.

Why today's options fail them

  • ✕Cloud API — needs a connection; the data leaves the premises.
  • ✕"Secured" cloud (VPC / zero-retention / TEE) — still connected. Fails the unplugged test.
  • ✕70B local on a GPU — offline, but a visible quality gap, and a box to babysit.
  • ✕Self-host 753B on 8×H100 — ~$250–400k + kW power + MLOps. A build, not an appliance.
02

The product: a frontier model at a seat price, not a datacenter price

A single-user box (an FPGA card) that streams the whole quantized model from an NVMe SSD through a fast DDR cache into a Q4_K compute die. Everything lives on the box — model, cache, compute — and the host link carries only token IDs, so it runs with the ethernet unplugged. Deliberately memory-bandwidth-bound to be cheap: the NVMe holds the 467 GB Q4_K model, the die stays small, speed comes from memory bandwidth — not an expensive datacenter GPU. Performance is staged by hardware (see the ladder below).

verification-first

Every "proven" row is reproducible from the repo (make q4k, make formal, make model-q4k, make resident). The self-referential caveat is closed at the dequant layer: our reimplementation is bitwise-equal to llama.cpp's own dequantize_row_q4_K/_q6_K on a real published GGUF's raw blocks — so by transitivity the RTL dequant ≡ the real file's ggml dequant. 04

The path to silicon: the FPGA-fit gate

▍ Next gate

The biggest hardware unknown — "does the die fit a real, affordable FPGA?" — is now measured, not estimated: the Q4_K product top places and routes on the named ~$1,000-class-board part. What's left is the physical board demo.

The FPGA fit sets the box's BOM, and the BOM sets the per-seat price the whole business rests on. The compute datapath elaborates at full 753B scale and the whole chip passes the structural sign-off (yosys check-assert, 0 unresolved) — the design is coherent end-to-end, not a sketch.

The target part is named — a Kintex UltraScale+ XCKU3P (1,368 DSP48E2, DDR4 + PCIe) — and the buildable proof is priced: a ~$1,000-class dev board, not a research program. The vendor-tool gate is done: the Q4_K product top places and routes on the XCKU3P (87.5% LUT, routed 46.5 MHz after a bit-exact repipeline campaign). The fit was measured at a pinned commit and carried forward by proof, not promise: RTL changes since are formally proven netlist-identical at the fit configuration (yosys sequential equivalence), or the fit re-runs. What remains is the board itself running real weights — that demo converts the roofline [EST]s into a measured tok/s.
